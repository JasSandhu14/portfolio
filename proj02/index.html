Proposition: The United States is one of the leading nations in terms of Human Capital Index

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Highlighted the United States to emphasize it against most countries, differentiating it against the bulk of countries. Many countries also pack together in the bottom left, showing the US as superior to the masses. (1.5)
  • Removed Luxembourg, Ireland, Switzerland, Singapore, and Norway as they had higher rankings. Their removal showed the US being greater than other nations, building on the previous design decision. (-1.5)
  • Compare the US GDP per capita and HCI, instead of just HCI as HCI alone isn't that impressive. As you can still see, many nations still have a greater HCI even after the removal of the some countries. However, with GDP per capita, the US is shown to be further removed from the pack. (-0.5)

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Highlighted China as it is considered to be the greatest rival to the US. This comparison makes the US look worse as it fall short against its greatest competitor. (-0.5)
  • Labeled Hong Kong as China as mainland China has a worse score than the US. However Hong Kong's score is considerably greater. (-2.0)
  • Used a line graph instead of a scatter to show the recent sharp decline in the United States. The drastic fall off from 2017 to 2018 is very clear, especially since the line is colored blue. (0.5)
  • Specifically selected many "popular" countries with greater scores than the United States to show the US lacking when compared to other first world nations. Then selected "smaller" nations with lesser scores to show how close the US is to these nations. (-1.0)

Final Reflection

The most challenging portion of this project was manipulating data in a way that benefited against the stance I was arguing. Coming in, I had a stance I wished to argue, and such, had an idea for data to use. However the opposite proved to be more difficult as I had to transform the data to serve this argument. Yet, as I continued to explore the data, I found convincing ways to twist the narrative. The straightforward portions of this assignment were the use of deceptive tricks. From the initial plots I created, I would adjust them slightly to serve the purpose I wished for. For example, for Figure 1, I plotted data from all given countries in the dataset. From there, I would remove the countries whose dots surpassed the US.

This project showcased the thin line that ethical analysis and visualizations can have. By manipulating, removing, or reformatting data, I can transform the viewers interpretation to what I wish. Many of these deceptive tactics may be seen as intentionally manipulative, such as mislabeling countries like I did for Figure 2. However, many others can be seen as genuine persuasive techniques, like highlighting a single instance to differentiate it from many, shown with Figure 1. Ultimately, it is a personal ethical decision of which methods one wants to use for their persuasion, as no bounds can define what is acceptable for all analysis and visualization scenarios.
